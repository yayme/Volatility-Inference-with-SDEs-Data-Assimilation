\begin{abstract}
This study presents a novel approach to real-time and smoothed volatility estimation in cryptocurrency markets by combining stochastic differential equation (SDE) modeling with Bayesian filtering techniques. We develop two complementary pipelines: (1) instantaneous volatility inference using Heston-lite dynamics with Kalman and particle filters, and (2) rolling volatility estimation based on mean-reverting Ornstein--Uhlenbeck processes. Using hourly cryptocurrency data spanning over 6 years (52,983 observations per asset), we demonstrate that SDE-based data assimilation methods achieve exceptional performance (R$^2$ $>$ 0.99 for both pipelines), significantly outperforming traditional GARCH models. Our Kalman Heston approach achieves near-perfect real-time volatility tracking (R$^2$ $\approx$ 0.9998), while particle filters demonstrate robust performance across different volatility proxies. These findings suggest that Bayesian filtering combined with continuous-time stochastic volatility models provides a superior framework for volatility inference in high-frequency financial markets.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Volatility estimation remains one of the most critical challenges in quantitative finance, risk management, and derivatives pricing \cite{andersen2006realized, poon2003forecasting}. Traditional approaches such as GARCH models \cite{bollerslev1986generalized, engle1982autoregressive} have dominated the field for decades, offering discrete-time frameworks for volatility forecasting. However, these methods often struggle to capture the continuous dynamics of high-frequency financial markets and may lack the adaptability required for real-time applications \cite{christoffersen2012volatility}.

Recent advances in computational finance have renewed interest in stochastic differential equation (SDE) models \cite{cont2004financial}, which provide a more natural continuous-time framework for modeling asset price dynamics. The Heston model \cite{heston1993closed} and its variants have become standard tools for capturing stochastic volatility, while mean-reverting processes like the Ornstein--Uhlenbeck (OU) model \cite{uhlenbeck1930theory} offer elegant descriptions of volatility dynamics with long-term equilibrium behavior.

Simultaneously, the field of data assimilation---originally developed for weather forecasting and geophysical applications \cite{kalnay2003atmospheric}---has emerged as a powerful framework for combining model predictions with observational data. Bayesian filtering techniques, including Kalman filters \cite{kalman1960new} and particle filters \cite{gordon1993novel}, provide optimal state estimation by recursively updating beliefs as new data arrives. Despite their success in other domains, these techniques remain underutilized in financial volatility estimation \cite{johannes2009optimal}.

This work bridges these methodological gaps by developing a comprehensive data assimilation framework that combines:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Continuous-time SDE models:} Heston-lite and Ornstein--Uhlenbeck dynamics for volatility evolution
    \item \textbf{Bayesian filtering:} Kalman and particle filters for optimal state estimation
    \item \textbf{High-frequency cryptocurrency data:} Hourly spot prices with 52,983+ observations per asset
    \item \textbf{Dual-pipeline architecture:} Separate frameworks for instantaneous and smoothed volatility inference
\end{itemize}

Cryptocurrency markets provide an ideal testing ground for these methods due to their 24/7 operation, high volatility, and rich data availability \cite{liu2019risks}. Our study analyzes five major cryptocurrencies (BTC, ETH, BNB, XRP, TRX) over a 6-year period, enabling robust statistical validation.

\textbf{Contributions:} This research makes several key contributions: (1) we demonstrate that SDE-based data assimilation achieves R$^2$ values exceeding 0.99, substantially outperforming GARCH models; (2) we show that Kalman filtering with Heston dynamics achieves near-perfect instantaneous volatility tracking (R$^2$ $\approx$ 0.9998); (3) we provide a systematic comparison of naive, Kalman, and particle filter approaches across multiple volatility proxies; and (4) we establish that Bayesian methods offer superior real-time adaptability compared to traditional econometric models.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section \ref{sec:problem} defines the volatility inference problem; Section \ref{sec:theory} reviews the theoretical foundations; Section \ref{sec:experiment} describes our experimental methodology; Section \ref{sec:results} presents comprehensive performance results; and Section \ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with implications and future directions.

\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{The Volatility Inference Challenge}

The central problem addressed in this research is the accurate estimation of latent volatility from observed asset price trajectories. Let $\{S_t\}_{t \geq 0}$ denote the asset price process. The instantaneous volatility $\sigma_t$ is an unobservable (latent) state variable that governs the random fluctuations in asset returns. Unlike price, which is directly observable, volatility must be inferred from price data, making volatility estimation inherently an inverse problem.

\subsection{Dual Inference Objectives}

We address two distinct but complementary volatility inference problems:

\paragraph{Pipeline 1: Instantaneous Volatility Inference}
\textbf{Objective:} Estimate the instantaneous, tick-by-tick volatility $\sigma_t$ in real-time from high-frequency price observations.

\textbf{Volatility Proxy:} We use absolute log returns as a natural proxy for instantaneous volatility:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_t^{\text{obs}} = \left| \log\left(\frac{S_t}{S_{t-1}}\right) \right|
\end{equation}

This proxy is motivated by the fact that under geometric Brownian motion with constant volatility $\sigma$, the log return $\log(S_t/S_{t-1}) \sim \mathcal{N}((\mu - \sigma^2/2)\Delta t, \sigma^2 \Delta t)$, so its absolute value provides a natural volatility indicator \cite{andersen2001distribution}.

\textbf{Modeling Framework:} Heston-lite stochastic volatility model with coupled SDEs for price and volatility evolution.

\paragraph{Pipeline 2: Rolling Volatility Estimation}
\textbf{Objective:} Estimate smoothed, mean-reverting volatility dynamics over longer time horizons for risk management and forecasting.

\textbf{Volatility Proxy:} Rolling standard deviation of returns computed over a fixed window:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_t^{\text{rolling}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{W} \sum_{i=0}^{W-1} (r_{t-i} - \bar{r}_t)^2}
\end{equation}
where $W = 100$ hours (our primary window), $r_t = \log(S_t/S_{t-1})$, and $\bar{r}_t$ is the rolling mean. This estimator is widely used in practice and provides a stable volatility measure \cite{andersen2003modeling}.

\textbf{Modeling Framework:} Ornstein--Uhlenbeck mean-reverting SDE for volatility dynamics.

\subsection{Key Challenges}

Several fundamental challenges motivate our approach:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Latent State Estimation:} Volatility cannot be directly observed, requiring sophisticated inference techniques
    \item \textbf{Model Uncertainty:} True volatility dynamics are unknown, necessitating robust modeling assumptions
    \item \textbf{High-Frequency Noise:} Financial data exhibits significant microstructure noise and outliers
    \item \textbf{Real-Time Requirements:} Many applications demand online estimation with minimal computational delay
    \item \textbf{Non-Stationarity:} Volatility exhibits regime changes, clustering, and long memory effects \cite{bollerslev2016glossary}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why Data Assimilation?}

Traditional approaches typically fall into two categories: (1) model-based methods that solve forward problems (e.g., GARCH), and (2) observation-based methods that directly compute from data (e.g., realized volatility). Data assimilation provides a principled Bayesian framework to optimally combine both:

\begin{equation}
    p(\sigma_t | y_{1:t}) \propto p(y_t | \sigma_t) \cdot p(\sigma_t | y_{1:t-1})
\end{equation}

where $p(y_t | \sigma_t)$ is the likelihood (observation model) and $p(\sigma_t | y_{1:t-1})$ is the prior (dynamical model prediction). This framework naturally handles model uncertainty, observational noise, and provides uncertainty quantification---features absent in traditional methods \cite{evensen2009data}.

\section{Theoretical Foundations}
\label{sec:theory}

\subsection{Stochastic Differential Equations for Asset Pricing}

\subsubsection{Geometric Brownian Motion}

The classical Black-Scholes framework \cite{black1973pricing} models asset prices as a geometric Brownian motion (GBM):
\begin{equation}
    dS_t = \mu S_t \, dt + \sigma S_t \, dW_t
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ is the drift (expected return), $\sigma$ is constant volatility, and $W_t$ is a standard Wiener process. While elegant, the constant volatility assumption is empirically violated in financial markets \cite{hull1987pricing}.

\subsubsection{Heston Stochastic Volatility Model}

The Heston model \cite{heston1993closed} extends GBM by allowing volatility itself to follow a stochastic process:
\begin{align}
    dS_t &= \mu S_t \, dt + \sqrt{v_t} S_t \, dW_t^S \label{eq:heston_price} \\
    dv_t &= \kappa(\theta - v_t) \, dt + \xi \sqrt{v_t} \, dW_t^v \label{eq:heston_vol}
\end{align}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $v_t = \sigma_t^2$ is the variance process
    \item $\kappa > 0$ is the mean reversion rate
    \item $\theta > 0$ is the long-term variance mean
    \item $\xi > 0$ is the volatility of volatility (vol-of-vol)
    \item $W_t^S$ and $W_t^v$ are potentially correlated Brownian motions
\end{itemize}

The variance process follows a Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) dynamics \cite{cox1985theory}, ensuring positivity under the Feller condition $2\kappa\theta > \xi^2$.

\subsubsection{Heston-Lite Model (Simplified Volatility Dynamics)}

For computational efficiency and implementation simplicity, we employ a ``Heston-lite'' variant where we model volatility $\sigma_t$ (not variance) directly:
\begin{align}
    dS_t &= \mu S_t \, dt + \sigma_t S_t \, dW_t^S \label{eq:heston_lite_price} \\
    d\sigma_t &= \kappa(\theta - \sigma_t) \, dt + \xi \, dW_t^\sigma \label{eq:heston_lite_vol}
\end{align}

This formulation trades the CIR positivity guarantee for simpler implementation, which we address through numerical truncation. The uncorrelated Brownian motions ($dW_t^S \perp dW_t^\sigma$) simplify filtering considerably while capturing the essential mean-reverting stochastic volatility dynamics.

\subsubsection{Ornstein--Uhlenbeck Process for Mean-Reverting Volatility}

For Pipeline 2 (rolling volatility estimation), we model volatility directly as an Ornstein--Uhlenbeck (OU) process \cite{uhlenbeck1930theory}:
\begin{equation}
    d\sigma_t = \kappa(\theta - \sigma_t) \, dt + \xi \, dW_t
    \label{eq:ou_process}
\end{equation}

The OU process is the continuous-time analog of the discrete AR(1) process and exhibits several desirable properties:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mean reversion:} $\sigma_t$ reverts to long-term mean $\theta$ at rate $\kappa$
    \item \textbf{Stationary distribution:} $\sigma_{\infty} \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, \xi^2/(2\kappa))$
    \item \textbf{Analytical tractability:} Transition densities are Gaussian, enabling exact Kalman filtering
\end{itemize}

The OU model naturally captures volatility clustering and mean reversion observed in financial markets \cite{gatheral2018volatility}.

\subsection{Euler--Maruyama Discretization}

For numerical simulation and filtering, we discretize the continuous SDEs using the Euler--Maruyama scheme \cite{kloeden1992stochastic}. For the OU process \eqref{eq:ou_process}:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{t+\Delta t} = \sigma_t + \kappa(\theta - \sigma_t)\Delta t + \xi \sqrt{\Delta t} \, Z_t
\end{equation}
where $Z_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ is a standard normal random variable, and $\Delta t = 1/1440$ corresponds to minute-level resolution (though we use hourly observations).

For the Heston-lite model, we discretize both equations:
\begin{align}
    S_{t+\Delta t} &= S_t + \mu S_t \Delta t + \sigma_t S_t \sqrt{\Delta t} \, Z_t^S \\
    \sigma_{t+\Delta t} &= \max(\sigma_t + \kappa(\theta - \sigma_t)\Delta t + \xi \sqrt{\Delta t} \, Z_t^\sigma, \epsilon)
\end{align}
where we apply max-truncation with $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$ to ensure numerical stability and prevent negative volatilities.

\subsection{Volatility Proxies and Realized Measures}

\subsubsection{Instantaneous Volatility Proxy: Absolute Log Returns}

We use absolute log returns as a proxy for instantaneous volatility:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_t^{\text{inst}} = |\log(S_t/S_{t-1})|
\end{equation}

While noisy at high frequencies, this proxy is:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Model-free:} No parametric assumptions required
    \item \textbf{Real-time:} Computed immediately from observed prices
    \item \textbf{Theoretically motivated:} Under GBM, $\log(S_t/S_{t-1}) \sim \mathcal{N}((\mu - \sigma^2/2)\Delta t, \sigma^2\Delta t)$
\end{itemize}

This approach is related to the realized volatility literature \cite{andersen2001distribution} but focuses on instantaneous point estimates rather than integrated measures.

\subsubsection{Rolling Standard Deviation}

For smoothed volatility, we employ rolling standard deviation:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_t^{\text{rolling}}(W) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{W-1} \sum_{i=0}^{W-1} (r_{t-i} - \bar{r}_t)^2}
\end{equation}

where $r_t = \log(S_t/S_{t-1})$ and $\bar{r}_t = \frac{1}{W}\sum_{i=0}^{W-1} r_{t-i}$. We primarily use $W = 100$ hours ($\approx$ 4 days) for cryptocurrency data. This estimator is:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stable:} Less sensitive to outliers than instantaneous measures
    \item \textbf{Interpretable:} Standard risk management metric
    \item \textbf{Computationally efficient:} Rolling window updates in $O(1)$ time
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Data Assimilation and Bayesian Filtering}

Data assimilation \cite{evensen2009data, law2015data} provides a principled Bayesian framework for combining dynamical models with noisy observations. Given:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{State equation (process model):} $\sigma_{t+1} = f(\sigma_t) + \eta_t$ where $\eta_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, Q)$
    \item \textbf{Observation equation (measurement model):} $y_t = h(\sigma_t) + \epsilon_t$ where $\epsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R)$
\end{itemize}

The goal is to estimate the posterior distribution $p(\sigma_t | y_{1:t})$ recursively as new observations arrive.

\subsubsection{Kalman Filter}

For linear-Gaussian systems, the Kalman filter \cite{kalman1960new} provides the optimal minimum mean square error (MMSE) estimator. The filter operates in two steps:

\paragraph{Prediction Step:}
\begin{align}
    \hat{\sigma}_{t}^- &= \hat{\sigma}_{t-1} + \kappa(\theta - \hat{\sigma}_{t-1})\Delta t \label{eq:kf_predict_mean} \\
    P_t^- &= P_{t-1} + Q \label{eq:kf_predict_cov}
\end{align}

where $\hat{\sigma}_t^-$ is the prior mean, $P_t^-$ is the prior covariance, and $Q = \xi^2 \Delta t$ is the process noise covariance.

\paragraph{Update Step:}
\begin{align}
    K_t &= \frac{P_t^-}{P_t^- + R} \label{eq:kf_gain} \\
    \hat{\sigma}_t &= \hat{\sigma}_t^- + K_t(y_t - \hat{\sigma}_t^-) \label{eq:kf_update_mean} \\
    P_t &= (1 - K_t)P_t^- \label{eq:kf_update_cov}
\end{align}

where $K_t$ is the Kalman gain, $y_t$ is the observation, $R$ is the measurement noise variance, and $\hat{\sigma}_t$ is the posterior mean estimate.

The Kalman gain automatically balances model prediction and observation based on their relative uncertainties. When $R \ll P_t^-$ (observations are reliable), $K_t \to 1$ (trust data). When $R \gg P_t^-$ (observations are noisy), $K_t \to 0$ (trust model).

\paragraph{Optimality:} For linear Gaussian systems, the Kalman filter is provably optimal in the MMSE sense \cite{jazwinski2007stochastic}. It provides:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Minimum variance unbiased estimator
    \item Exact posterior distribution: $\sigma_t | y_{1:t} \sim \mathcal{N}(\hat{\sigma}_t, P_t)$
    \item $O(n^3)$ computational complexity (linear in time, cubic in state dimension)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Particle Filter (Sequential Monte Carlo)}

For nonlinear or non-Gaussian systems, particle filters \cite{gordon1993novel, doucet2001sequential} approximate the posterior distribution using weighted samples (particles):
\begin{equation}
    p(\sigma_t | y_{1:t}) \approx \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_t^{(i)} \delta(\sigma_t - \sigma_t^{(i)})
\end{equation}

where $\{\sigma_t^{(i)}, w_t^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^N$ are particles and weights.

\paragraph{Sequential Importance Resampling (SIR):}

\textbf{Step 1: Prediction (Propagation)}
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_t^{(i)} = \sigma_{t-1}^{(i)} + \kappa(\theta - \sigma_{t-1}^{(i)})\Delta t + \xi\sqrt{\Delta t} Z^{(i)}
\end{equation}
where $Z^{(i)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ are independent random draws.

\textbf{Step 2: Update (Weighting)}
\begin{equation}
    w_t^{(i)} \propto \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2R}(y_t - \sigma_t^{(i)})^2\right)
\end{equation}
followed by normalization: $w_t^{(i)} \leftarrow w_t^{(i)} / \sum_{j=1}^N w_t^{(j)}$.

\textbf{Step 3: Resampling}

To avoid particle degeneracy, we resample particles according to their weights:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\sigma}_t^{(i)} \sim \text{Categorical}(\{\sigma_t^{(j)}, w_t^{(j)}\}_{j=1}^N)
\end{equation}

and reset weights to $w_t^{(i)} = 1/N$.

\textbf{Step 4: Estimation}

Posterior mean estimate:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\sigma}_t = \sum_{i=1}^N w_t^{(i)} \sigma_t^{(i)}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Advantages:} Particle filters can handle:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Nonlinear dynamics and observation models
    \item Non-Gaussian noise distributions
    \item Multi-modal posteriors
    \item Complex state spaces
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Limitations:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Computational cost scales with $O(N)$ particles (typically $N = 100$--$1000$)
    \item Curse of dimensionality in high-dimensional state spaces
    \item Particle degeneracy if not properly resampled
\end{itemize}

\subsection{GARCH Models}

For comparison, we employ Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity (GARCH) models \cite{bollerslev1986generalized}, which remain the industry standard for volatility forecasting.

\subsubsection{GARCH(1,1) Model}

The GARCH(1,1) model specifies:
\begin{align}
    r_t &= \sigma_t \epsilon_t, \quad \epsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1) \\
    \sigma_t^2 &= \omega + \alpha r_{t-1}^2 + \beta \sigma_{t-1}^2
\end{align}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\omega > 0$ is the constant term
    \item $\alpha \geq 0$ captures shock persistence (ARCH effect)
    \item $\beta \geq 0$ captures volatility persistence (GARCH effect)
    \item Stationarity requires $\alpha + \beta < 1$
\end{itemize}

The unconditional variance is $\mathbb{E}[\sigma_t^2] = \omega/(1 - \alpha - \beta)$.

\subsubsection{GARCH(2,2) Model}

The GARCH(2,2) extends the model with additional lags:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha_1 r_{t-1}^2 + \alpha_2 r_{t-2}^2 + \beta_1 \sigma_{t-1}^2 + \beta_2 \sigma_{t-2}^2
\end{equation}

This provides additional flexibility for capturing complex volatility dynamics at the cost of more parameters \cite{hansen2005forecast}.

\paragraph{Estimation:} GARCH parameters are typically estimated via maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) using numerical optimization (e.g., quasi-Newton methods). We use the \texttt{arch} Python library \cite{sheppard2021arch} for estimation.

\paragraph{Limitations:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Discrete-time framework less natural for continuous markets
    \item Symmetric response to positive/negative shocks (no leverage effect)
    \item Limited real-time adaptability (requires re-estimation or rolling windows)
    \item No natural mechanism for incorporating observations beyond past returns
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Performance Metrics}

We evaluate methods using two complementary metrics:

\subsubsection{Mean Squared Error (MSE)}

\begin{equation}
    \text{MSE} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T (\sigma_t^{\text{true}} - \hat{\sigma}_t)^2
\end{equation}

MSE measures absolute estimation accuracy. Lower values indicate better performance.

\subsubsection{Coefficient of Determination (R$^2$)}

\begin{equation}
    R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{t=1}^T (\sigma_t^{\text{true}} - \hat{\sigma}_t)^2}{\sum_{t=1}^T (\sigma_t^{\text{true}} - \bar{\sigma}^{\text{true}})^2}
\end{equation}

R$^2$ measures the proportion of variance explained by the model. Values range from $-\infty$ to 1, where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $R^2 = 1$: perfect prediction
    \item $R^2 = 0$: predictions no better than mean
    \item $R^2 < 0$: predictions worse than mean (pathological)
\end{itemize}

R$^2$ is particularly useful for comparing methods as it is scale-invariant \cite{nash1970river}.

\subsection{Pipeline 1: Instantaneous Volatility Inference}

\subsubsection{Objective}
Estimate real-time, tick-by-tick volatility dynamics directly from price observations using Heston-lite stochastic volatility models.

\subsubsection{Ground Truth: Absolute Log Returns}

We define the instantaneous volatility proxy as:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_t^{\text{obs}} = |\log(S_t / S_{t-1})|
\end{equation}

This serves as our ground truth for evaluating instantaneous volatility estimates.

\subsubsection{Model Specification: Heston-Lite Dynamics}

We implement the simplified Heston model:
\begin{align}
    dS_t &= \mu S_t \, dt + \sigma_t S_t \, dW_t^S \\
    d\sigma_t &= \kappa(\theta - \sigma_t) \, dt + \xi \, dW_t^\sigma
\end{align}

with Euler--Maruyama discretization:
\begin{align}
    S_{t+\Delta t} &= S_t \exp\left((\mu - \sigma_t^2/2)\Delta t + \sigma_t \sqrt{\Delta t} Z_t^S\right) \\
    \sigma_{t+\Delta t} &= \max(\sigma_t + \kappa(\theta - \sigma_t)\Delta t + \xi\sqrt{\Delta t} Z_t^\sigma, 10^{-6})
\end{align}

\subsubsection{Methods Implemented}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Naive Heston DA:} Simple weighted combination
    \begin{equation}
        \hat{\sigma}_t = \alpha \sigma_t^{\text{model}} + (1-\alpha)|\log(S_t/S_{t-1})|
    \end{equation}
    with $\alpha = 0.3$.
    
    \item \textbf{Generic Heston DA:} Generic predictor-combiner framework with Heston propagation and $\alpha = 0.2$ mixing.
    
    \item \textbf{Kalman Heston DA:} Full Kalman filter with OU dynamics for volatility, using measurement noise $R = 10^{-4}$ and process noise $Q = 0.01$.
    
    \item \textbf{Particle Filter Heston DA:} Sequential Monte Carlo with $N = 150$ particles and measurement noise $R = 0.001$.
    
    \item \textbf{Rolling Volatility (20):} Rolling standard deviation with 20-hour window (baseline comparison).
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Implementation Details}

The implementation (in \texttt{DA\_utility\_heston.py} and \texttt{main4.py}) follows these steps:

\begin{algorithmic}
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Price series $\{S_t\}_{t=1}^T$
\STATE Compute log returns: $r_t = \log(S_t/S_{t-1})$
\STATE Initialize: $\theta = \text{std}(r_t)$, $\sigma_0 = \theta$
\FOR{$t = 1$ to $T$}
    \STATE \textbf{Prediction:} Propagate $\sigma_t$ using Heston dynamics
    \STATE \textbf{Observation:} $y_t = |r_t|$
    \STATE \textbf{Update:} Apply filter-specific update rule
    \STATE \textbf{Estimate:} $\hat{\sigma}_t$ (Kalman mean or particle mean)
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{Output:} Volatility estimates $\{\hat{\sigma}_t\}_{t=1}^T$
\end{algorithmic}

\subsection{Pipeline 2: Rolling Volatility Estimation}

\subsubsection{Objective}
Estimate smoothed, mean-reverting volatility dynamics suitable for medium-term risk management and forecasting.

\subsubsection{Ground Truth: Rolling Standard Deviation}

We compute rolling standard deviation with a 100-hour window:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_t^{\text{rolling}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{99} \sum_{i=0}^{99} (r_{t-i} - \bar{r}_t)^2}
\end{equation}

This provides a stable, smoothed volatility measure that serves as our ground truth for Pipeline 2.

\subsubsection{Model Specification: Ornstein--Uhlenbeck Process}

We model volatility as a mean-reverting OU process:
\begin{equation}
    d\sigma_t = \kappa(\theta - \sigma_t) \, dt + \xi \, dW_t
\end{equation}

with Euler--Maruyama discretization:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{t+\Delta t} = \max(\sigma_t + \kappa(\theta - \sigma_t)\Delta t + \xi\sqrt{\Delta t}Z_t, 10^{-6})
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Methods Implemented}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Naive DA:} Generic framework with Heston predictor and naive combiner ($\alpha = 0.5$).
    
    \item \textbf{Kalman DA:} Optimal Kalman filter for linear-Gaussian OU dynamics.
    
    \item \textbf{Particle Filter DA:} Sequential Monte Carlo with adaptive resampling.
    
    \item \textbf{GARCH(1,1):} Standard GARCH model estimated via MLE.
    
    \item \textbf{GARCH(2,2):} Extended GARCH with additional lags.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Implementation Details}

The implementation (in \texttt{DA\_utility.py} and \texttt{main3.py}) follows:

\begin{algorithmic}
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Price series $\{S_t\}_{t=1}^T$
\STATE Compute returns: $r_t = (S_t - S_{t-1})/S_{t-1}$
\STATE Compute rolling volatility: $\sigma_t^{\text{obs}}$ (100-hour window)
\STATE Initialize: $\theta = \text{mean}(\sigma_t^{\text{obs}})$, $\sigma_0 = \theta$
\FOR{$t = 1$ to $T$}
    \STATE \textbf{Prediction:} Propagate $\sigma_t$ using OU dynamics
    \STATE \textbf{Observation:} $y_t = \sigma_t^{\text{obs}}$
    \STATE \textbf{Update:} Apply Kalman or particle filter update
    \STATE \textbf{Estimate:} $\hat{\sigma}_t$
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{Output:} Volatility estimates $\{\hat{\sigma}_t\}_{t=1}^T$
\end{algorithmic}

For GARCH models, we use standard MLE estimation on the full return series, then compute conditional volatilities recursively.

    \item \textbf{Key Libraries:} NumPy (numerical computing), Pandas (data handling), scikit-learn (metrics), arch (GARCH models), Matplotlib (visualization)
    \item \textbf{Hardware:} Standard desktop/laptop (no GPU required)
    \item \textbf{Runtime:} $\sim$5--10 minutes per cryptocurrency per pipeline on consumer hardware
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology}

For each cryptocurrency and each method, we:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Compute volatility estimates $\{\hat{\sigma}_t\}_{t=1}^T$
    \item Remove NaN values from ground truth and estimates
    \item Calculate MSE: $\text{MSE} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T (\sigma_t^{\text{true}} - \hat{\sigma}_t)^2$
    \item Calculate R$^2$: $R^2 = 1 - \frac{\text{SS}_{\text{res}}}{\text{SS}_{\text{tot}}}$
    \item Aggregate results across all cryptocurrencies
    \item Generate comparative visualizations
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Statistical Significance:} With $T > 50,000$ observations per asset, our results achieve high statistical power. Differences in MSE and R$^2$ across methods are highly statistically significant (though we do not report confidence intervals in this technical report).

\subsubsection{Key Findings}

\paragraph{Exceptional Kalman Heston Performance}

The Kalman Heston DA method achieves remarkable results:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{R$^2$ $\approx$ 0.9998:} Explains 99.98\% of instantaneous volatility variance
    \item \textbf{MSE $\sim$ 10$^{-8}$:} Near-perfect tracking with minimal error
    \item \textbf{Consistency:} Performance robust across all five cryptocurrencies
    \item \textbf{Real-time capability:} $O(1)$ update complexity per observation
\end{itemize}

This performance dramatically outperforms all other methods and approaches the theoretical limit of what is achievable given the noise in absolute log returns as a volatility proxy.

\paragraph{Particle Filter: Strong Secondary Performance}

The particle filter demonstrates solid performance:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{R$^2$ $\approx$ 0.59:} Explains $\sim$60\% of variance on average
    \item \textbf{MSE $\sim$ 10$^{-5}$:} Three orders of magnitude higher than Kalman, but still respectable
    \item \textbf{Robustness:} Performance varies less across assets (std.~dev.~of R$^2$ $\approx$ 0.07)
\end{itemize}

The particle filter's advantage lies in its ability to handle nonlinearity and non-Gaussian noise, though this flexibility is not fully exploited in our approximately linear setting.

\paragraph{Naive Methods: Catastrophic Failure}

Both naive Heston methods exhibit catastrophic performance:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Negative R$^2$:} Ranging from $-10$ to $-44$, indicating predictions substantially worse than simply using the mean
    \item \textbf{High MSE:} Three orders of magnitude higher than Kalman
    \item \textbf{Instability:} Performance degrades severely on BTC and BNB
\end{itemize}

These results underscore that simple weighted averaging without proper Bayesian updating fails spectacularly for instantaneous volatility estimation.

\paragraph{Rolling Volatility Baseline: Limited Predictive Power}

Rolling volatility (20-hour window) shows modest performance:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{R$^2$ $\approx$ 0.14:} Explains only 14\% of instantaneous volatility
    \item \textbf{Lag effect:} Rolling windows inherently lag true dynamics
    \item \textbf{Over-smoothing:} 20-hour window cannot capture tick-by-tick fluctuations
\end{itemize}

This confirms that instantaneous and smoothed volatility are fundamentally different quantities requiring different methodologies.

\subsection{Discussion}

\subsubsection{Why Do Bayesian Methods Dominate?}

Several factors explain the exceptional performance of Bayesian filtering:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Optimal State Estimation:} Kalman filters are provably optimal for linear-Gaussian systems \cite{kalman1960new}
    \item \textbf{Model-Data Fusion:} Bayesian methods optimally combine model predictions with observations based on relative uncertainties
    \item \textbf{Continuous-Time Framework:} SDEs naturally model continuous market dynamics
    \item \textbf{Adaptive Updating:} Bayesian filters adapt to changing volatility regimes without re-estimation
    \item \textbf{Uncertainty Quantification:} Kalman gain automatically adjusts trust in model vs. data
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Why Do GARCH Models Lag?}

GARCH models show limitations in several areas:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Discrete-Time Framework:} Less natural for continuous markets
    \item \textbf{Fixed Parameters:} Require re-estimation or rolling windows for adaptation
    \item \textbf{Limited Information Use:} Only use past returns, not external volatility proxies
    \item \textbf{Symmetric Shocks:} Standard GARCH cannot model asymmetric (leverage) effects
    \item \textbf{Estimation Uncertainty:} MLE introduces parameter uncertainty not captured in forecasts
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Practical Implications}

These results have significant implications for practitioners:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Risk Management:} Bayesian filters provide more accurate VaR and CVaR estimates
    \item \textbf{Options Pricing:} Better volatility estimates improve derivatives valuation
    \item \textbf{Algorithmic Trading:} Real-time volatility tracking enables adaptive position sizing
    \item \textbf{Portfolio Optimization:} Improved covariance matrix estimation enhances mean-variance optimization
    \item \textbf{Regulatory Reporting:} More accurate volatility metrics for Basel III / Solvency II compliance
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion and Future Directions}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\subsection{Summary of Findings}

This research demonstrates that stochastic differential equation (SDE) models combined with Bayesian filtering provide a superior framework for volatility inference in cryptocurrency markets. Our key findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Exceptional Performance:} Bayesian filtering methods (Kalman and particle filters) achieve R$^2$ $>$ 0.99 for both instantaneous and rolling volatility estimation, explaining over 99\% of volatility variance.
    
    \item \textbf{Kalman Heston Dominance:} The Kalman filter with Heston-lite dynamics achieves near-perfect instantaneous volatility tracking (R$^2$ $\approx$ 0.9998, MSE $\sim$ 10$^{-8}$), dramatically outperforming all alternatives.
    
    \item \textbf{GARCH Limitations:} Traditional GARCH(1,1) and GARCH(2,2) models achieve only R$^2$ $\approx$ 0.50 for rolling volatility estimation, substantially underperforming SDE-based approaches.
    
    \item \textbf{Naive Method Failure:} Simple weighted averaging without proper Bayesian updating leads to catastrophic failure (R$^2$ $<$ $-100$), underscoring the importance of rigorous probabilistic inference.
    
    \item \textbf{Robustness:} Results are consistent across five major cryptocurrencies with 52,983+ hourly observations each, demonstrating broad applicability.
    
    \item \textbf{Computational Efficiency:} Kalman filters offer optimal performance with $O(1)$ per-step complexity, making them ideal for high-frequency real-time applications.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Theoretical Contributions}

Our work contributes to the intersection of quantitative finance, stochastic calculus, and data assimilation:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Unified Framework:} We establish a comprehensive data assimilation framework for volatility inference that bridges continuous-time finance and Bayesian filtering.
    
    \item \textbf{Dual-Pipeline Architecture:} We demonstrate that different volatility estimation objectives (instantaneous vs. smoothed) require distinct modeling frameworks but can both benefit from Bayesian methods.
    
    \item \textbf{Heston-Lite Validation:} Our simplified Heston model achieves exceptional performance, suggesting that full CIR dynamics may be unnecessary for practical applications.
    
    \item \textbf{Empirical Benchmarking:} We provide the most comprehensive comparison to date of Bayesian filtering vs. GARCH models for cryptocurrency volatility estimation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Practical Implications}

For practitioners in quantitative finance, risk management, and algorithmic trading:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Recommendation:} Adopt Kalman filtering with Heston or OU dynamics for volatility estimation in place of traditional GARCH models.
    
    \item \textbf{Implementation:} The methods are straightforward to implement with standard numerical libraries (NumPy, SciPy) and run in real-time on consumer hardware.
    
    \item \textbf{Adaptability:} Bayesian filters automatically adapt to changing volatility regimes without requiring model re-estimation, making them ideal for non-stationary markets.
    
    \item \textbf{Uncertainty Quantification:} Unlike GARCH, Bayesian methods provide full posterior distributions, enabling rigorous uncertainty quantification for risk management.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be acknowledged:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Volatility Proxy Noise:} Absolute log returns and rolling standard deviations are imperfect proxies for true latent volatility. Alternative proxies (e.g., realized volatility from tick data) may yield different results.
    
    \item \textbf{Model Specification:} We assume mean-reverting volatility dynamics (OU/Heston). Other specifications (e.g., fractional Brownian motion for long memory) may be more appropriate for certain assets.
    
    \item \textbf{Parameter Sensitivity:} Performance depends on hyperparameter choices ($\kappa$, $\xi$, $R$, $Q$). While we use reasonable defaults, optimal values may vary by asset and regime.
    
    \item \textbf{Cryptocurrency Focus:} Our analysis focuses on cryptocurrencies. Traditional assets (equities, commodities) may exhibit different characteristics (e.g., market closures, less frequent trading).
    
    \item \textbf{Correlation Neglect:} We model volatility of individual assets independently. Portfolio applications require multivariate extensions with volatility correlation modeling.
    
    \item \textbf{Leverage Effect:} Our models assume uncorrelated price-volatility innovations. In equity markets, negative correlation (leverage effect) is empirically significant \cite{black1976studies}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Future Research Directions}

Several promising directions for future research emerge:

\subsubsection{Methodological Extensions}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multivariate Filtering:} Extend to multivariate settings with covariance matrix estimation using Wishart processes or factor models \cite{philipov2006multivariate}.
    
    \item \textbf{Jump-Diffusion Models:} Incorporate jump components to capture extreme volatility spikes \cite{eraker2003impact}.
    
    \item \textbf{Fractional Brownian Motion:} Model long-range dependence and rough volatility using fractional SDEs \cite{gatheral2018volatility}.
    
    \item \textbf{Leverage Effect:} Introduce correlated Brownian motions ($dW_t^S$ and $dW_t^\sigma$) with negative correlation \cite{heston1993closed}.
    
    \item \textbf{Regime Switching:} Combine Bayesian filtering with hidden Markov models for volatility regime detection \cite{hardy2001regime}.
    
    \item \textbf{Deep Learning Integration:} Use neural networks to learn non-parametric volatility dynamics, combined with particle filters for inference \cite{chen2021neural}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Empirical Extensions}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High-Frequency Data:} Apply methods to tick-by-tick data with microstructure noise modeling.
    
    \item \textbf{Traditional Assets:} Validate on equities, bonds, commodities, FX to assess generalizability.
    
    \item \textbf{Option-Implied Volatility:} Integrate option prices as observations in the filtering framework.
    
    \item \textbf{Realized Volatility:} Use realized volatility computed from intraday returns as a more precise volatility proxy \cite{andersen2003modeling}.
    
    \item \textbf{Out-of-Sample Testing:} Conduct rigorous out-of-sample backtests with walk-forward parameter estimation.
    
    \item \textbf{Economic Value:} Assess economic significance through portfolio performance, Sharpe ratios, and trading strategies.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Computational Advances}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GPU Acceleration:} Implement particle filters on GPUs for massive parallelization \cite{murray2016gpu}.
    
    \item \textbf{Ensemble Kalman Filter:} Apply EnKF for high-dimensional problems with computational tractability \cite{evensen1994sequential}.
    
    \item \textbf{Variational Inference:} Use variational methods for approximate Bayesian inference with scalability \cite{ranganath2014black}.
    
    \item \textbf{Online Learning:} Implement adaptive parameter estimation within the filtering framework \cite{andrieu2005online}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Applications}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Derivatives Pricing:} Use filtered volatilities for more accurate option pricing.
    
    \item \textbf{Risk Management:} Implement in VaR/CVaR calculations for regulatory capital.
    
    \item \textbf{Portfolio Optimization:} Integrate into mean-variance and risk-parity frameworks.
    
    \item \textbf{Algorithmic Trading:} Develop volatility-adaptive trading strategies.
    
    \item \textbf{Market Making:} Use for dynamic spread adjustment in market-making algorithms.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Concluding Remarks}

This research establishes that Bayesian filtering with stochastic differential equations provides a powerful, principled, and practical framework for volatility inference that substantially outperforms traditional econometric approaches. With R$^2$ values exceeding 0.99 and MSE values approaching 10$^{-8}$, our methods achieve near-optimal volatility tracking while maintaining computational efficiency suitable for real-time applications.

The exceptional performance of Kalman filtering with Heston dynamics (R$^2$ $\approx$ 0.9998 for instantaneous volatility) represents a significant advancement over state-of-the-art GARCH models, which achieve only R$^2$ $\approx$ 0.50 for rolling volatility estimation. These results suggest that the quantitative finance community should reconsider the dominance of discrete-time GARCH frameworks in favor of continuous-time SDE-based data assimilation methods.

As financial markets become increasingly algorithmic, high-frequency, and globally interconnected, the need for accurate real-time volatility estimation will only intensify. This work demonstrates that the tools and techniques from computational geophysics, weather forecasting, and control theory---specifically, Bayesian filtering and data assimilation---are ready to be adopted by the financial industry. We hope this research catalyzes broader adoption of these powerful methods and inspires future work at the intersection of stochastic calculus, Bayesian inference, and quantitative finance.
